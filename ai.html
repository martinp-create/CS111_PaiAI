Folk of the Air — Holly Black

Thoughtful notes on The Cruel Prince, The Wicked King, and The Queen of Nothing

A short, stylish single-page overview

Overview & central characters

Holly Black's Folk of the Air trilogy follows Jude Duarte, a mortal girl raised in the treacherous world of Faerie. Beginning with The Cruel Prince, we are introduced to a court full of shifting loyalties, brutal politics, and a protagonist who chooses grit and cunning over pity. Jude's complicated relationship with Cardan — a prince who is at times cruel, enigmatic, and magnetic — becomes the emotional engine driving much of the trilogy's tension.

The books move from revenge and survival toward questions of power, belonging, and identity. Jude's choices repeatedly challenge both human and fae expectations: she learns to weaponize her vulnerabilities and to negotiate with monsters on terms that are often morally ambiguous. That moral ambiguity — where doing the 'right' thing can look like betrayal, and where loyalty is transactional — gives the series its edgy charge.

While royal intrigue and action deliver plot momentum, the series keeps returning to family and found-family dynamics. Jude's relationships with her sisters, her mentors, and even her enemies evolve in ways that complicate simple 'hero' and 'villain' labels, making the trilogy feel as much about personal growth as it is about crowns and conspiracies.

Themes, style, and why it resonates

Holly Black writes with lean, sharp prose that foregrounds atmosphere and action: the Faerie court is rendered as both gorgeous and cruel. The trilogy mixes elements of classic courtly fantasy with modern sensibilities — snappy dialogue, morally-grey choices, and a protagonist whose determination often reads as a quiet, stubborn feminism. Magic in these books is rarely kind; it is transactional, dangerous, and frequently exacts a price that complicates the narrative's victories.

Thematically, the trilogy explores the costs of ambition and the tension between power and ethics. Jude's ascent is thrilling because it never feels cost-free: victory and compromise are intertwined, and the consequences of political maneuvering are shown in intimate, personal ways. Readers who appreciate character-driven fantasy with sharp interpersonal dynamics and a willingness to let protagonists fail and learn will find much to love here.

Finally, the trilogy's pacing and arcs make it addictive: each book deepens the stakes, and by The Queen of Nothing the emotional and political threads tie together into a resonant, if sometimes messy, conclusion. Whether you come for the romance, the court intrigue, or the moral puzzles, Holly Black's trilogy rewards attention and re-reading — there are layers that reveal themselves on a second pass.
